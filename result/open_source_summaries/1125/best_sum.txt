The article discusses the importance of designing margin-based loss functions for face recognition, which can enhance the feature margin between different classes for better discriminability. The authors propose a novel Adaptive Curriculum Learning loss (CurricularFace) that embeds the idea of curriculum learning into the loss function to achieve a novel training strategy for deep face recognition. The CurricularFace adaptively adjusts the relative importance of easy and hard samples during different training stages, addressing easy samples in the early stage and hard ones in the later stage. This approach is motivated by the nature of human learning, where easy cases are learned first and then come the hard ones.

The authors compare their approach with existing methods, such as ArcFace and SV-Arc-Softmax, which do not explicitly emphasize the importance of each sample. They also discuss the drawbacks of these methods, including ignoring the difficultness of each sample and over-emphasizing hard samples in early training. The authors argue that their approach is more effective because it adapts to the difficulty of each sample and adjusts the importance of hard samples in different training stages.

The CurricularFace is designed to be easy to implement and robust to converge, and extensive experiments on popular facial benchmarks demonstrate its superiority over the state-of-the-art competitors. The authors also discuss the potential for future research, including designing better functions for negative cosine similarity and investigating the effects of noise samples.
